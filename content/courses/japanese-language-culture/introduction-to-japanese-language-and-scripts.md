# Introduction to Japanese Language & Writing Systems

## Overview

:::aeo
Teaching tip: emphasize pronunciation and short practice drills.
:::
Japanese is not just a language — it is a **reflection of culture, social behavior, and thought patterns**.  
This lesson introduces the **core foundations** of Japanese language learning, focusing on:

- The relationship between **language and culture**
- The **three Japanese writing systems**
- Pronunciation structure and syllables
- Numbers and basic greetings

This lesson sets the stage for all future learning.

---

## Language and Culture Are Deeply Connected

Japanese expressions often **do not translate directly** into English.  
Why?

Because Japanese language is shaped by:

- Social hierarchy
- Politeness levels
- Group harmony
- Cultural context

Many phrases make sense **only within Japanese culture**, which is why learning Japanese requires cultural awareness alongside grammar and vocabulary.

---

## Why Japanese Writing Is Unique

Japanese uses **three writing systems simultaneously**:

| Script       | Purpose                                      |
| ------------ | -------------------------------------------- |
| **Hiragana** | Native Japanese words, grammar, verb endings |
| **Katakana** | Foreign and loan words                       |
| **Kanji**    | Meaning-based characters (ideas & objects)   |

A single sentence may contain **all three scripts**, with **no spaces between words**.

---

## The Origin of Japanese Scripts

### Before the 4th Century

- Japanese existed as a **spoken language only**
- There was **no writing system**

### Influence of Trade

- Chinese characters entered Japan via **trade with China and Korea**
- These characters evolved into **Kanji**

### Birth of Kana Scripts

- **Hiragana** developed for Japanese-origin words
- **Katakana** developed for foreign-origin words

---

## Hiragana and Katakana: Phonetic Scripts

Both Hiragana and Katakana are **phonetic**, meaning:

> _You write exactly what you say._

### Core Facts

- 46 basic characters each
- Additional modified characters using diacritical marks
- Same pronunciation, **different visual style**

### Visual Difference

- **Hiragana** → Rounded, cursive, flowing
- **Katakana** → Angular, sharp, rigid

---

## Japanese Pronunciation Rules

### Vowels (The Foundation)

Japanese pronunciation is built on **five vowels**:

| Vowel | Sound |
| ----- | ----- |
| あ    | a     |
| い    | i     |
| う    | u     |
| え    | e     |
| お    | o     |

### Syllable Rule (Very Important)

> Every Japanese syllable **ends in a vowel**,  
> **except one**: **ん (n)**

Examples:

- ka, ki, ku, ke, ko
- sa, shi, su, se, so
- Only exception: _n_

---

## Exceptions You Must Memorize

Some sounds do **not follow English expectations**:

| Written | Pronounced |
| ------- | ---------- |
| し      | _shi_      |
| ち      | _chi_      |
| つ      | _tsu_      |

These must be learned through **practice and repetition**.

---

## Katakana and Foreign Words

Katakana is used for:

- Foreign words
- Loanwords
- Non-Japanese names

Examples:

- コーヒー (_kōhī_) → Coffee
- ケーキ (_kēki_) → Cake
- コーラ (_kōra_) → Cola

### Important Pronunciation Note

Japanese **does not have the "L" sound**.

- All **L sounds become R**
- Example: _Cola_ → _Kora_

---

## Introduction to Kanji (Ideograms & Pictograms)

Kanji characters represent **ideas or objects**, not sounds.

### Simple Examples

| Concept | Meaning   |
| ------- | --------- |
| 日      | Sun / Day |
| 人      | Person    |
| 水      | Water     |
| 火      | Fire      |
| 山      | Mountain  |

Kanji often combine to form **new meanings**:

- 火 + 山 → Volcano (Fire Mountain)

---

## Stroke Order Matters

Kanji must be written in a **fixed stroke order**.

Why?

- Ensures correct character structure
- Enables faster writing
- Helps readability and recognition

Stroke order is **not optional** — it is essential.

---

## How Japanese Is Written

Japanese can be written:

- **Vertically** (right → left)
- **Horizontally** (left → right)

Modern Japanese typically uses **all three scripts together**, without spaces.

## Everyday Greetings

### Good Morning

- **Ohayō gozaimasu** (formal)
- **Ohayō** (informal)

Used from **early morning until around 10 AM**

### Good Day

- **Konnichiwa**

Used from **late morning until early evening**

---

## Learning Advice

To learn Japanese effectively:

- Speak **out loud**
- Repeat sounds until comfortable
- Focus on **listening and pronunciation**
- Practice scripts daily, even briefly

> Hearing yourself speak helps identify mistakes faster than silent reading.
