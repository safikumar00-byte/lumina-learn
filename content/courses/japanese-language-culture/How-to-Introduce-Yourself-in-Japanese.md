How to Introduce Yourself in Japanese (Hiragana, Particles & Basic Conversation)

To introduce yourself in Japanese, you say: Hajimemashite. Watashi wa [Name] desu. Yoroshiku onegaishimasu. This structure uses basic hiragana, the particle wa, and the polite ending desu. In this lesson, you will also learn how to form simple questions using ka and expand your pronunciation with new hiragana sounds.

:::
To introduce yourself in Japanese, say: “Hajimemashite. Watashi wa [Your Name] desu. Yoroshiku onegaishimasu.”
This means: “Nice to meet you. I am [Name]. Please treat me kindly.”
:::

Expanding Hiragana: T, N, and H Series

Japanese has 46 basic hiragana characters, each representing one sound.

In this lesson, we extend beyond vowels, K-series, and S-series to include:

T-Series

ta

chi (exception: not “ti”)

tsu (exception: not “tu”)

te

to

:::
“Chi” and “tsu” are pronunciation exceptions in the T-series.
:::

N-Series

na

ni

nu

ne

no

H-Series

ha

hi

fu (pronounced softly between “hu” and “fu”)

he

ho

:::
Hiragana: A Japanese phonetic script used for native Japanese words and grammatical endings.
:::

Practice reading these aloud to train your mouth and ear to Japanese rhythm.

Why Japanese Uses Three Scripts

Japanese writing combines:

Hiragana (phonetic)

Katakana (foreign words)

Kanji (Chinese-origin characters)

Japanese does not use spaces between words. Multiple scripts help readers distinguish word boundaries.

:::
English: Uses spaces to separate words.
Japanese: Uses script variation to visually separate meaning.
:::

Basic Self-Introduction Structure
Step 1: Say “Nice to meet you”

Hajimemashite

:::
Hajimemashite: A greeting used only when meeting someone for the first time.
:::

Step 2: State Your Name

Watashi wa [Name] desu.

Example:

Watashi wa Tanaka desu.
(I am Tanaka.)

:::
Wa (は): A topic particle marking what the sentence is about.
:::

Sentence structure:

Subject + wa + complement + desu

Japanese follows a Subject–Object–Verb pattern, and verbs typically appear at the end.

Step 3: End Politely

Yoroshiku onegaishimasu

This expresses goodwill and asks for positive future relations.

Combined introduction:

Hajimemashite.
Watashi wa Tanaka desu.
Yoroshiku onegaishimasu.

Talking About Your Profession

Replace your name with your profession:

Watashi wa gakusei desu. (I am a student.)
Watashi wa sensei desu. (I am a teacher.)
Watashi wa isha desu. (I am a doctor.)

Pattern remains unchanged.

Making Questions with “Ka”

To turn a statement into a question, add ka at the end.

Statement:
Anata wa gakusei desu. (You are a student.)

Question:
Anata wa gakusei desu ka? (Are you a student?)

:::
Ka: A particle placed at the end of a sentence to make it a question.
:::

Important Note

In real conversation, instead of “anata” (you), Japanese speakers typically use the person’s name.

Example:
Tanaka-san wa gakusei desu ka?

Short Answers

Hai. (Yes.)
Hai, sō desu. (Yes, that’s correct.)

Informal (friends only):
Sō.

Checking Understanding

Wakari mashita ka?
(Have you understood?)

Answer:
Hai, wakari mashita.

Basic Kanji: Numbers 1–3

You are introduced to three fundamental kanji:

Ichi (1)

Ni (2)

San (3)

Stroke order matters. Each character follows a specific writing pattern.

Counting from 11 to 30

11–19 follow pattern:

Ju + number

Example:

Juichi (11)

Jūni (12)

Jūsan (13)

20: Nijū
21: Nijūichi
30: Sanjū

Understanding this pattern simplifies counting significantly.
