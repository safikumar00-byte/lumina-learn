How Do You Introduce Yourself in Japanese?

To introduce yourself in Japanese, you say Hajimemashite, followed by
Watashi wa [your name] desu, and end with Yoroshiku onegaishimasu.
This polite structure is used when meeting someone for the first time.

:::
To introduce yourself in Japanese, say: "Hajimemashite. Watashi wa [name] desu.
Yoroshiku onegaishimasu." This means "Nice to meet you. I am [name]. Please treat me well."
It is the standard formal self-introduction used in Japan.
:::

Key Phrases for Self-Introduction

1. Hajimemashite (はじめまして)

Used only when meeting someone for the first time.
It literally means "beginning," implying the start of a relationship.

2. Watashi wa [Name] desu (わたし は \_\_\_ です)

Watashi = I

wa (は) = topic particle

[Name] = your name

desu = polite copula ("am/is")

Example:

Watashi wa Tanaka desu.
I am Tanaka.

:::
The particle wa (は) marks the topic of a sentence in Japanese. It follows a noun
or pronoun and indicates what the sentence is about.
:::

3. Yoroshiku onegaishimasu (よろしく おねがいします)

A polite closing phrase meaning:

"Please treat me well."

"I look forward to working with you."

"I place myself in your care."

It expresses goodwill and respect.

Introducing Your Profession

You can replace your name with your profession.

Pattern:

Watashi wa [profession] desu.

Examples:

Watashi wa gakusei desu. (I am a student.)

Watashi wa sensei desu. (I am a teacher.)

Watashi wa bengoshi desu. (I am a lawyer.)

:::
Hajimemashite.
Watashi wa Kumar desu.
Watashi wa gakusei desu.
Yoroshiku onegaishimasu.

Nice to meet you. I am Kumar. I am a student. Please treat me well.
:::

Asking Questions Using "ka"

To turn a statement into a question, simply add ka (か) at the end.

Statement:

Watashi wa gakusei desu.
I am a student.

Question:

Gakusei desu ka?
Are you a student?

Important notes:

"Ka" makes a sentence interrogative.

Use rising intonation when speaking.

Instead of saying anata (you), use the person's name.

Example:

Tanaka-san wa gakusei desu ka?

Basic Hiragana Series Covered

Hiragana contains 46 basic characters. In this lesson, we reviewed:

Ta Series

ta, chi, tsu, te, to
Note: chi and tsu are pronunciation exceptions.

Na Series

na, ni, nu, ne, no

Ha Series

ha, hi, fu, he, ho
"Fu" is pronounced softly, between "hu" and "fu."

Practicing aloud helps build correct pronunciation and fluency.

Introduction to Basic Kanji Numbers

We also introduced three essential kanji:

一 (ichi) – one

二 (ni) – two

三 (san) – three

Stroke order matters:

Ichi: one horizontal stroke

Ni: short line above, longer line below

San: three horizontal lines, increasing length

These foundational kanji form the basis of Japanese counting.

Common Expressions
Mata ashita aimashou (また あした あいましょう)

Mata = again

Ashita = tomorrow

Aimashou = let's meet

Meaning: "Let's meet again tomorrow."

Konbanwa (こんばんは)

Means "Good evening."
