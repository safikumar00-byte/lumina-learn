# Hiragana M, Y, R Series, Nationalities & Basic Question Patterns

In this lesson, you will learn the Hiragana M (ま-み-む-め-も), Y (や-ゆ-よ), and R (ら-り-る-れ-ろ) series, how to form negative sentences, ask where someone is from, express nationality using **–jin**, use **kara** for origin, practice number patterns (31–50), and write basic kanji numbers with correct stroke order.

---

## Hiragana: M Series (ま・み・む・め・も)

The M series combines the consonant **M** with the five vowels:

| Hiragana | Romaji | Example         | Meaning |
| -------- | ------ | --------------- | ------- |
| ま       | ma     | まくら (makura) | pillow  |
| み       | mi     | みみ (mimi)     | ear     |
| む       | mu     | むぎ (mugi)     | wheat   |
| め       | me     | め (me)         | eye     |
| も       | mo     | もも (momo)     | peach   |

Practice pronunciation repeatedly to build fluency.

---

## Hiragana: Y Series (や・ゆ・よ)

The Y series only has three sounds:

| Hiragana | Romaji | Example        | Meaning    |
| -------- | ------ | -------------- | ---------- |
| や       | ya     | やさい (yasai) | vegetables |
| ゆ       | yu     | ゆき (yuki)    | snow       |
| よ       | yo     | よる (yoru)    | night      |

> There are no standalone “yi” or “ye” sounds in modern standard Japanese.

---

## Hiragana: R Series (ら・り・る・れ・ろ)

| Hiragana | Romaji | Example            | Meaning |
| -------- | ------ | ------------------ | ------- |
| ら       | ra     | らいおん (raion)   | lion    |
| り       | ri     | りんご (ringo)     | apple   |
| る       | ru     | ルール (ruuru)     | rule    |
| れ       | re     | れもん (remon)     | lemon   |
| ろ       | ro     | ロボット (robotto) | robot   |

## Forming Questions and Negatives

### Basic Question Structure

**Noun + wa + noun + desu ka?**

Example:

Kumar-san wa gakusei desu ka?  
Is Kumar a student?

### Positive Answer

Hai, Kumar-san wa gakusei desu.

### Negative Form

Noun + wa + noun + **ja arimasen**

Kumar-san wa gakusei ja arimasen.  
Kumar is not a student.

---

## Asking “Where Are You From?”

To ask someone where they are from:

**Okuni wa dochira desu ka?**  
(Where are you from? — polite)

To answer:

Watashi wa America kara desu.  
I am from America.

### Understanding “kara”

**Kara** means “from” when attached to a place.

India kara  
From India

Japan kara  
From Japan

---

## Expressing Nationality with –jin

To say your nationality:

Watashi wa India-jin desu.  
I am Indian.

Watashi wa Nihon-jin desu.  
I am Japanese.

Watashi wa America-jin desu.  
I am American.

The suffix **–jin** means “person of that country.”

### What is the difference between _kara_ and _–jin_?

- **Kara** expresses origin (from a place).
  - Watashi wa India kara desu.
- **–jin** expresses nationality (citizenship/identity).
  - Watashi wa India-jin desu.

Use _kara_ for location origin and _–jin_ for nationality.

---

## Using “ka” for Choice (OR)

You can use **ka** between two nouns to mean “or.”

Pen desu ka enpitsu desu ka?  
Is it a pen or a pencil?

Taiwan kara desu ka, Chugoku kara desu ka?  
Are you from Taiwan or China?

Structure:

Noun 1 + desu ka + Noun 2 + desu ka?

---

## Asking Someone’s Name

Onamae wa nan desu ka?  
What is your name?

Answer:

Watashi wa Kim desu.

---

## Numbers 31–50

31–40 follow the pattern:

Sanjuu + number

- 31 = Sanjuu ichi
- 35 = Sanjuu go
- 40 = Yonjuu

41–50 follow:

Yonjuu + number

- 41 = Yonjuu ichi
- 45 = Yonjuu go
- 50 = Gojuu

---

## Kanji: Stroke Order for Numbers 4–6

Correct stroke order is essential. Kanji are generally written:

- Top to bottom
- Left to right
- Ending in the lower right corner

### Four (四)

5 strokes  
Readings: **yon** / **shi**

### Five (五)

4 strokes  
Reading: **go**

### Six (六)

4 strokes  
Reading: **roku**

Practice writing carefully and consistently.
